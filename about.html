The Story

Why We
Built This.

I Belong started with a simple observation — the best players aren't the ones who study the most. They're the ones who study with the right people.

♠   ♥   ♣   ♦

"Poker gave me a lot of things — discipline, focus, the ability to think clearly under pressure. But the thing it couldn't give me on its own was the right room."

I spent years studying alone. Books, videos, solvers — all of it solo. I was improving, but slowly, and I always felt like I was missing something. The feedback loop was broken. I had no one to challenge my thinking, no one to tell me when I was rationalizing a bad call, no one who cared as much about the game as I did.

When I finally found people like that — serious players who wanted to study together, who held each other accountable, who could spot a leak I'd been blind to for months — everything accelerated. The community was the variable I'd been missing.

I Belong exists because that kind of community shouldn't be hard to find. It should be the first thing you build when you decide to take this game seriously.

If you're here, you already know what I'm talking about. There's a seat waiting for you.

What We Believe

The principles
behind everything.

♠
Community compounds growth.

Solo study has a ceiling. The players who improve the fastest are in rooms where their thinking gets challenged, their leaks get spotted, and their wins get celebrated by people who understand them.

♥
Process over results.

Poker is a long game. We don't chase results — we build the processes that produce them. Good decisions lead to good outcomes over enough time. We trust that.

♣
Quality over quantity.

A small room of serious players is worth more than a large room of casual ones. We'd rather have 50 members who are all in than 500 who are just along for the ride.

♦
Honesty is the edge.

The most valuable thing a community can offer is honest feedback. Not validation — real, specific, sometimes uncomfortable feedback that actually makes you better. We don't sugarcoat here.
